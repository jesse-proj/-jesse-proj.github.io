Chromatography

The foundation of chromatography is the idea that mixtures of molecules applied to surfaces or solids, and fluid stationary phases (stable phases), separate from one another while moving with the help of a mobile phase. The molecular features linked to adsorption (liquid-solid), partition (liquid-solid), and affinity or variations among their molecular weights are the factors that have an impact on this separation process. These variations lead certain combination components to spend more time in the stationary phase and travel more slowly through the chromatography system, while others pass quickly into the mobile phase and exit the system more quickly.

The separation of a mixture/substance fused with a mobile phase as it flows and passes through a stationary phase. An Example of Chromatography (leaf extract) A column is filled up with calcium carbonate, afterwards, the leaf extract mixed with ethanol and ether is added. As the leaf extract passes through the calcium carbonate, the chlorophyll pigments start to separate in different levels. In this example, the stationary phase is the calcium carbonate. Meanwhile, the mobile phase is the ethanol and ether. The substance being identified for this example is the leaf extract.

Paper Chromatograpy

  • Used commonly with amino acids, DNA bases, and amino acids
  • Paper stands as the solid supporting material
  • Separating phase (water molecules)
  • Mobile phase (ethanol/methanol/chloroform)

Liquid Chromatography

  • Uses a solid stationary phase with small pores to separate particles
  • LC uses a liquid substance for the mobile phase.

Gas Chromatography

  • Used for separating compunds that are volatile
  • Mobile phase (inert gas, ex: Helium, Nitrogen, Argon)
  • Stationary phase (usually silicon polymers)
